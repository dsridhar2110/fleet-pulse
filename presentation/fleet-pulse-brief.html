01

Problem

The role is data science for Siemens Healthineers' service organisation — the team that keeps 250,000+ imaging machines (MRI, CT, X-ray) running worldwide. When a scanner fails unexpectedly, a hospital cancels 20–40 scans that day (~$27k lost), and Siemens dispatches an engineer reactively. The team's job is to read the machines' own logs and predict failures before they happen. Fleet Pulse builds exactly that capability, end to end, on realistic synthetic data.

Data specification

A simulated fleet built from real failure physics — parts wear out on age & usage curves, and degradation leaks into sensors before failure. Enough structure to learn, kept deliberately hard.

EntityVolumeDetail
Machines500CT, MRI, X-ray across 10 countries, install years 2015–2024
Telemetry~1,041,000 rowsDaily sensor vitals: helium, temperatures, tube current, vibration
Error events323,000Mostly benign noise; only certain families precede failure
Service tickets1,610Free-text engineer notes (deliberately messy/inconsistent)
Failures (labels)440~1.7% of machine-weeks; 15% give no early warning
Feature table24,867 × 226One row per machine per week; 226 engineered signals
05

Model specification

PredictionProbability a machine fails within 7 days
GrainOne score per machine, per week
ModelXGBoost
ValidationTime-based split (train past → test future)
Leakage controlFeatures use only data up to scoring day; asserted by tests
ExplainabilitySHAP per-machine drivers

Measured as service decisions, not accuracy

  • Precision@20 — of the 20 machines flagged each week, how many visits pay off.
  • Lead time — how many days before failure the alert fires (must be days, not hours).
  • Calibration — a 0.6 score genuinely means a 60% chance.
  • Cost threshold — alarm tuned so a missed failure (~100× a visit) is weighted correctly.

08

Explaining it to Deekshita

Lead with the business framing; hand her the technical choices so she owns the interview defense.

  • The one-liner: “It's data science for their service business — predicting which scanner breaks next. I built a working product so you walk in already speaking their language.”
  • The engine is real: failures from Weibull wear-out physics, time-based split, calibrated scores, PR-AUC + precision@k, SHAP explanations, PySpark → XGBoost → DuckDB.
  • Her interview line on the synthetic data: “With your real fleet, my first two weeks would be with service engineers finding where my assumptions are wrong.” — humility + domain respect, which the JD explicitly asks for.
